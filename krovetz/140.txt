chapter i 
the ephemeri and its use 


an ephemeri is an almanac of the planetary position day by day throughout any given year. they are to be 
obtain at a cost of one shilling per year, in a convenient form specially adapt to the use of astrologers. in 
the ﬁrst column will be found the sidereal time for the day, which is the sun’s right ascension or distance from 
the vernal equinox, express in hours, minutes, and seconds, and equate to mean time at greenwich. by 
merely add the time after noon, or subtract the hour and minutes before noon, at which the birth took 
place, you will obtain the right ascension of the midheaven at the moment of birth. the use to which this is put 
will appear in the next chapter. it correspond to a particular degree of the zodiac which is in the midheaven at 
the given time of birth. 
the next column contain the sun’s longitude at noon for each day. by taking the position on one day fromthat [pg 35] 

on the next day, the motion of the sun for 24 hour is obtained, and a proportion of this for the hour of birth can 
easily be made. the mean motion of the sun per day is 2½′, or 1 degree per day. in another column will be 
found the moon’s longitude, and in adjacent column its declination and latitude. declination is distance north or 
south of the equator, and celestial latitude is distance north or south of the ecliptic. the longitude of the other 
body are also given for each day at noon. 
the mean longitudinal progress of the several body is as follows: sun,2½′ per hour, or 1 degree per day; 
moon, 32′ per hour, or 13 degree per day; neptune, 2′ per day; uranus, 3′ per day; saturn, about 5′ per day; 
jupiter, about 12′ per day; mars, 45′ per day, or about 2′ per hour; venus, 72′ per day, or 3′ per hour; mercury, 
84′ per day, or 3½′ per hour. these increment are for direct motion only; the planet are, as already explained, 
sometimes retrograde and sometimes stationary. the horoscope of birth is only concerned with the longitude 
of the planets, but when one or more planet have the same declination north or south, they should be noted 
as being in parallel, for they then act as if they were in conjunction. 
take in hand, then, the ephemeri for the year of your birth and read this chapter with it in view. after 
understanding its construction, extract the sidereal time at noon for the day of your birth and refer to the next 
chapter.

to draw a figure of the heavens. 
take a pair of compasses, and with a radius, of about 2½ inches, describe a circle. within this, and with a radius 
of about 2 inches, describe another. with the radius of this latter mark oﬀ six equal divisions, and then equally [pg 38] 

divide these so as to make twelve. within the ﬁgure draw a small circle, concentric with the others, to represent 
the earth. from the points of division already obtain draw straight lines to the centre of the ﬁgure until they 
touch the circle of the earth. you then have a ﬁgure which represent the house of the heavens, being a 
twelvefold division of the prime vertical. the lines extend from the outer circle to the earth are the cusp of 
the house (see fig. section iv., chap. 5). 
the degree of the zodiac which are upon the cusp of the houses, as found in the table opposite a given 
sidereal time, are written over the several cusp in their order between the two outer circles. reference to the 
ephemeri for may 1, 1890, show the sun at noon in taurus 11 degree 2 minutes. three and a half hour 
later it will have advanced about 8 minutes in longitude, so that its position will be in taurus 11 degree 10 
minutes. this longitude falls in the eighth house, and we therefore place the symbol of the sun in that division 
of the heaven and place against it the ﬁgures 11.10, so that as aries 26 is on the cusp of the eighth and 
taurus 26 on the cusp of the ninth house, the sun’s position in taurus 11.10 will fall between these cusps, i.e., 
in the eighth house. the rest of the planet are placed in the figure according to their respective longitudes.

chapter iii 
the table of house 


the table of the house on pp. 40, 41, are calculate for the latitude of 51° 32′ north. this will serve for 
london and environs, and approximate for the south of england and the midlands. table of house for other 
latitudes may be obtain through the booksellers, a useful set of half a dozen diﬀerent latitudes being sold for 
one shilling. 
in order to illustrate the use to which these table may be put in the solution of astronomical problems, let us 
take the sun’s position in the horoscope for the 1st of may, 1890, and ﬁnd when the sun set on that day on the 
horizon of london. the sun is in taurus, 11 degrees, and it will set when the opposite point of the heaven is 
rising. find under the column marked “ascen.” the 11th degree of scorpio, and against it in the column marked 
“sidereal time” you will ﬁnd 9h. 49m. 9s. this will be the right ascension of the midheaven when the sun set 
on london. we have already seen that the sidereal time at noon on the 1st of may was 2h. 37m. 28s., and 
this, taken from the former sidereal time, give about 7.12 p.m. as the time of sunset. now in the example 
horoscope the birth took place at 3.30 p.m., and this time, taken from 7.12 p.m., give 3h. 42m. from birth to 
sunset. if you multiply this by 15 to turn it into degree and minutes of the equator, you will obtain 55° 30′, which 
are the number of degree which pass over the midheaven from birth until sunset. at the rate of 1 degree for 
every year of life, this correspond to 55½ years of age, when the subject of this horoscope would come under 
the adverse inﬂuence of the sun opposition ascendant, and would suﬀer some serious ill health of a feverish 
nature, incident to the throat (the part rule by taurus, the sign in which the sun is situate). this is how 
prediction are made, but of this more anon.

if i wish to know what particular part of the zodiac one is subject to in any particular year of life—that is to [pg 42] 

say, what part of the zodiac is rising—i add 1 degree for every year to the midheaven at birth and thus obtain a 
new midheaven, under which, in the table of houses, i ﬁnd the ascendant which rule the year. thus in the 
case of one born on the 1st of may at 3.30 p.m. the 30th year come under the midheaven of leo 2, which is 30 
degree further advanced than cancer 2, and under leo 2 in the 10th house i ﬁnd the ascendant to be in libra 
24.7, which is the ascendant governing that year of life. the meaning and importance of these calculation will 
appear at a later stage of this exposition. 
when a degree is on the midheaven or the 10th house it is said to “culminate,” and when on the horizon 
eastward it is said to “ascend.” 
the midheaven is often call the “m.c.” (medium coeli), and the opposite point is call the “i.c.” (imaum 
coeli). they are the points of the ecliptic which cut through the meridian of a place at any given time.
